First Contact

Talking to aliens using nothing but math.

Module: Number Sense & the Coordinate Plane Topic: Ordered pairs · Factors 1 class period (55 min) Groups of 3–4

The big idea

Two groups of "civilizations" have to send a real message to each other — but they share no language. No words, no letters, no labeled pictures. The only thing they can agree on is math, because two dots means two dots whether you live in Ohio or Andromeda. Groups bootstrap a shared code from counting, then use the coordinate plane to transmit a hidden picture. This is exactly how scientists built the real 1974 Arecibo message beamed into space.

Why it lands in this room: the receiving group starts confused — that's step one, how they know they're engaged. Then what do you know. Then try something. The confusion is the curriculum.

Standards & objectives

Learning intention

I can encode and decode information using only numbers and the coordinate plane — no words allowed.

Standards touched

  • 6.NS.C.6c — plot ordered pairs; order matters.
  • 6.NS.B.4 — factors & multiples (deciding the grid size).
  • SMP 1, 3, 6 — persevere, argue from evidence, attend to precision.

Success criteria

  • I can build a number system from tally marks so another group learns my digits.
  • I can define + and = using only examples.
  • I can transmit a picture as ordered pairs (row, column).
  • I can decode another group's message and name where meaning broke down.

Teacher background: the bootstrapping ladder

You can't use a symbol until you've taught it, and the only thing you're allowed to assume is that a mark repeated means "count." Everything else is built on top, one rung at a time:

RungWhat you sendWhat the receiver figures out
1 · Digits• , •• , ••• , ••••"These marks are counting numbers."
2 · Equals••• ▢ •••The middle symbol ▢ means "is the same as" ( = ).
3 · Plus•• △ ••• ▢ •••••△ means "combine" ( + ).
4 · The gridthe single number 4949 = 7×7 → build a 7-by-7 board (see note).
5 · The pictureordered pairs like (••, •••)Fill row 2, column 3. Repeat until the image appears.

The 49 trick (real Arecibo move): the Arecibo message used a number with exactly two prime factors so aliens would know to arrange the bits in a rectangle. We use 49: its only sensible factor pair is 7 × 7, which forces a square grid with no ambiguity. Let students discover that — don't hand it to them.

The one rule

No words. No letters. No labeled drawings. Every symbol you use must be taught with math examples before you use it to mean anything.

A transmission strip that would get "rejected by the aliens": anything with a written word, an arrow with a caption, or a drawn picture of the answer. If it isn't numbers and symbols-you-defined, it doesn't fly.

Run of show (55 minutes)

0–10 min

1 · Warm-up (the 2 + 3)

Full warm-up is on page 3, formatted for your control panel / Notion. Q5 surfaces the exact misconception that will break their transmissions: order matters — (4,1) is not (1,4). Don't resolve it yet. Let it hang.

10–15 min

2 · Hook — the real message

Say: "In 1974 humans pointed the biggest radio dish on Earth at a star cluster 25,000 light-years away and sent a message. No English — because why would aliens know English? They sent math. Today you're doing the same thing, across the room."

Prompt: "If you had to tell someone something and you couldn't use any word or language they'd know… what's the one thing you're sure they'd also understand?" (Steer toward: counting / numbers.)
15–25 min

3 · Mini-lesson — climb the ladder together

Build rungs 1–3 on the board with the class so everyone owns the shared code before groups split off. Do it as a puzzle, not a lecture:

  • Put • , •• , ••• up. "What am I doing?" → counting.
  • Write ••• ▢ •••. "What could the box mean?" → equals.
  • Write •• △ ••• ▢ •••••. "Now the triangle?" → plus.
Teacher move: the instant a kid says "ohhh the box means equals," name it: "You were confused 40 seconds ago. That confusion is what made you look for the pattern. That's the whole job today."
25–43 min

4 · Work time — encode your message

Each group gets a Secret Image card (page 6) and blank grids + transmission strips. Their task:

  1. Agree, as a group, on their symbols for the digits, =, and + (they may reuse the class ones or invent their own — but then they must teach them on the strip).
  2. Send the grid size as a number to factor (49), so the receivers build the right board.
  3. List every filled square of their secret image as an ordered pair (row, column) in their number symbols.
Circulate for the misconception: the groups that don't nail down "row first or column first?" will transmit a sideways/flipped picture. Let it happen. It's the best teaching moment of the day and it's their discovery in Phase 5.
43–50 min

5 · Exchange & decode

Groups swap only their transmission strips (never the Secret Image card). Receivers decode onto a fresh 7×7 grid and shade the squares. No talking between groups.

Prompt on the board: "You are the aliens now. Step one: you're confused. Good. Step two: what do you know? Step three: try something."
50–55 min

6 · The reveal (exit ticket)

Sending group holds up the original Secret Image next to the decoded grid. Then each student answers the exit ticket (page 3):

  • Did the picture come through? If it was flipped or scrambled, what assumption caused it?
  • What's one thing both groups had to agree on that they never said out loud?

Differentiation & extensions

Support

  • Give struggling groups the Heart or Smiley (symmetric — a flip still looks right, lowering the stakes).
  • Pre-fill rungs 1–3 on their transmission strip so they only build the picture.
  • Shrink to a 5×5 grid (send the number 25 = 5×5).

Stretch

  • Give the Abbie or Rocket (not symmetric — order errors show instantly).
  • Ban the class symbols: the group must invent and teach a brand-new code from scratch.
  • Encode the picture as one long binary string (row by row, filled=•, empty=nothing) instead of coordinate pairs — closer to the real Arecibo bitmap.
  • Add a second "message": send a number fact about themselves (group size, today's date as a sum).

Warm-up & Exit Ticket

Warm-up — First Contact (2 review + 3 current)

Music during warm-up; alert at 0:30, countdown from 10.

Review (fluency):

  1. 4 × 6 = ______
  2. 35 ÷ 5 = ______

Today's thinking:

  1. On a grid, plot the point (3, 2). Which do you count first — across or up?
  2. List all the factor pairs of 49. Which pair would make a square?
  3. (Discuss) Is the point (4, 1) the same as (1, 4)? Convince your neighbor.

Target misconception (Q5): that (4,1) and (1,4) are "basically the same." They are not — order is the invisible agreement that makes the whole message work. Surface it; don't correct it yet. It pays off in the reveal.
